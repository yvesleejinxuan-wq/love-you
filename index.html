Goal 6 Clean Water & Sanitation

Background

Millions of people suffer every year from diseases associated with unsafe drinking water, sanitation and hygiene. Water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) related diseases remain among the leading causes of death and they contribute to malnutrition. Each year, many children die due to diarrhoea linked to inadequate WASH.

UNICEF's contribution towards reaching this goal centres on bringing safe drinking water, sanitation and hygiene services to homes, schools and health centres so that children can grow and learn in a safe environment.

Importance

Safe drinking water, sanitation and hygiene are crucial to human health and well-being. Safe WASH is not only a prerequisite to health, but contributes to livelihoods, school attendance and dignity. It helps to create communities that are resillent to live in healthy environments.

Drinking unsafe water impairs health through illnesses such as cholera, which is stemmed from the bacterium Vibro cholerae that is transmitted primarily by consuming food or water contaminated with the feces of an infected person. Evidence suggests that improving service levels towards safely managed drinking water or sanitation such as regulated piped water or connections to sewers with wastewater treatment can dramatically improve overall health and reduce diseases.

Applications

Many countries have applied this SDG goal for the betterment of their people.
These are a few examples:

  • India (Swachh Bharat Mission)
    • On October 2, 2014, India's prime minister, Mr. Narendra Modi to improve focus on hygiene and achieve universal sanitation across the country. The first phase of the mission was implemented as a nationwide campaign, aimed at eliminating open defecation in rural areas.


  • Singapore (NEWater)
    • The NEWater process is the recycling of treated used water into ultra-clean and high-grade reclaimed water to move Singapore towards water sustainability. There are 3 stages to the process. NEWater is mainly used for industrial and air-con cooling purposes at water fabrication plants and commercial buildings.


  • Brazil (Programa de Aceleração do Crescimento)
    • Programa de Aceleração do Crescimento is a large-scale infrastructure and investment program launched by the Brazil government in 2007. PAC was introduced to modernize Brazil's infrastructure and overcome logistical bottlenecks that constrained growth. Clean water and sanitation is a priority in this project.

Impacts

  • Improved Health and Reduced Mortality:
    • Access to safe water and sanitation dramatically reduces child mortality and prevents the spread of diseases like cholera, dysentery, and typhoid.

  • Education and Gender Equality:
    • Improved sanitation in schools, especially for menstrual health, reduces absenteeism for girls, while reduced water-fetching duties allow more children to attend school.

  • Environmental Protection:
    • Effective sanitation protects ecosystems, biodiversity, and reduces pollution by properly managing wastewater, preventing the contamination of, for example, 3.4 billion people's water

  • Climate Change Resilience:
    • Future infrastructure will likely focus on solar-powered, sustainable water systems to mitigate the impact of droughts and floods.

Actions

Individual Actions:
  • Conserve Water: Fix leaks, take shorter showers, and turn off taps while brushing teeth.
  • Prevent Pollution: Do not pour oil, medicine, or chemicals down the drain or toilet.
  • Water Purification: Boil water for 1 to 3 minutes to kill germs. Use household filters, chlorination, or solar disinfection.
  • Improve Hygiene: Regularly wash hands with soap to prevent disease spread.

Community and Local Initiatives:
  • Cleanup Projects: Organize or join initiatives to clean local rivers, beaches, and water sources.
  • Water Education: Raise awareness about the importance of sanitation and hygiene.
  • Infrastructure Investment: Support the construction of public toilets, water treatment plants, and improved sewage systems.

Challenges

Environmental & Resource Challenges:
  • Water Scarcity: A problem worsened by population growth and increasing demand from agriculture and industry.
  • Pollution & Contamination: Water sources are frequently contaminated by untreated human waste, agricultural runoff (pesticides and fertilizers), and industrial discharge.
  • Climate Change Impacts: Climate change leads to droughts and floods. Flooding can damage infrastructure and contaminate clean water sources, while droughts deplete them.

Infrastructure & Management Challenges:
  • Inadequate Infrastructure: Many regions lack the necessary infrastructure, such as piped water to homes and proper sewage systems.
  • Insufficient Funding: A substantial funding gap exists between what is needed to achieve universal access to water and sanitation.
  • Poor Management & Governance: Challenges persist in the management and maintenance of existing water and sanitation systems, often due to weak institutional frameworks.

Social & Economic Challenges:
  • Inequality & Access: There are stark inequalities in access to safe water and sanitation, For example, overcrowding and inaccessibility for sanitation trucks.
  • Affordability: The cost of accessing water and sanitation services, or the means to treat water at home (e.g. boiling with fuel), can be prohibitively expensive for low-income populations.
  • Cultural & Behavioral Barriers: In some areas, cultural norms and lack of knowledge about disease transmission hinder the adoption of safe hygiene practices and the use of latrines.
